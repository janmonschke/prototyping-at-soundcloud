Prototyping is a term that gets thrown around a lot in many different situations.

Therefore there might be some confusion about what we actually mean when we speak about prototyping.

To make sure we're all on the same page I want to start by defining what prototyping is, at least, in terms of what it means in the context of this talk.

So what do we (Jan and I) mean when we say prototyping?

Prototyping is transforming the 'what-ifs' ideas in your head into something tangible. Something you can experience, work through, play with and test

You can think of a prototype as a simulation model of how the idea will work, how the application will flow, how the interaction works, how screens transition from one to another etc.

But why would we spend our time prototyping? How is it useful? Let‚Äôs take a look at a two examples where prototyping comes in handy.

High Fidelity Prototyping

A prototype that does not feel like a prototype.

High Fidelity Prototyping

  • A fully functioning app / site
  • Real production data
  • ‚ÄùSame‚Äù design like the real thing™
  • Users are presented with their real account data
  • ‚ÄùSimpler‚Äù implementation (e.g. performance)

Simple as in: Does not have all performance / load optimisations but is good enough for a user testing session

Why HiFi?

  • Immersion
  • Make use of familiarity
  • Less guidance / more freedom
  • Results more trustworthy / real
  • Fulfill expectations

Mention recently-played test where users where presented with static data. Expectation is key in those prototypes and when user's have an emotional connection to your product (e.g. they are collecting things on your platform) it is important to fulfill those emotional expectations.

real data === not-polished data (long usernames, weird artowrk, color schemes) that you just don't design with

Freedom as in users are free to do whatever and are not taken by their hands. You want them to express themselves freely.

Disadvantages of HiFi prototyping

How do we test this?

  • Users in the office
  • Diary studies
  • Internal testing

When we want feedback on the usability of something we're trying out, the concept and the design, we bring users in to the office to test out prototypes

One way we get feedback on our prototypes is to bring users into the office to test them

A series of regular in-house usability tests

We do it every three weeks to get regular unbiased feedback

Everyone involved in the feature participates, designers, engineers, product managers. Creates empathy with the users. We are actually very determined that everyone participates in the test - not just ux researchers.

Imagine the difference between me telling you the results from a usability test or you actually being there in a session seeing a user struggle with something? Which one are you more likely to remember?

Quick feedback: quickly test your designs

When we need people using the prototype in their own setting and over an extended period of time we do something we call a diary study

We give users a prototype to use with for around 2 weeks while checking in with us every other day

We do this when we need more than quick feedback

We also do a lot of informal internal testing

We distribute the prototypes company wide and we get back a lot of feedback that way

By doing this everyone can stay up to date with what product design is up to

It's also been a great way for us to finetune WIP recommendation algorithms

As you can maybe see by now - we've been doing prototyping for while however it has varied how we've done them, nowadays we mostly do everything in React Native - however that didn't use to be the case
